# Observer Frame Templates — Operational Reference (S4)

## Purpose

Observer frame embedding (Einstein's relativistic thinking applied to problems) re-examines the system from positions that have different information, different physical relations, and different measurement constraints than the default frame. Each frame reveals different imbalances, singularities, and limit cases.

---

## Frame 1 — Insider Frame

**Position:** An agent embedded within the system, subject to all its constraints, with only local information.

**What it reveals:** Local interactions, latency effects, information asymmetries, emergent behavior from inside the process.

**Embedding template:**
```
Insider perspective: I am [role inside the system]. I can only observe [local information]. 
I experience [specific process/interaction] directly.
From my position: what looks normal? What looks wrong? What information am I missing?
What constraints bind me that an outside observer would not notice?
Limit case from inside: What happens to me when [the system approaches its boundary condition]?
```

**Typical findings:** Bottlenecks that are invisible from outside, information that is lost at handoffs, local optima that prevent global optimization.

---

## Frame 2 — Outsider Frame

**Position:** An observer with no participation in the system — external observer who sees aggregate behavior but not internal mechanics.

**What it reveals:** Emergent properties, unexpected system-level behaviors, external dependencies, outputs and inputs that participants cannot see.

**Embedding template:**
```
Outsider perspective: I observe [the system] from outside and only see [inputs/outputs/aggregate behavior].
I cannot see [internal state, individual decisions, process mechanics].
From my position: what patterns are visible? What appears chaotic vs. structured?
What would I conclude is happening, without knowing the internal mechanism?
Limit case from outside: What happens to observable behavior when [the system is pushed to its boundary]?
```

**Typical findings:** The external appearance differs from internal intent, external dependencies that participants take for granted, black-box behavior patterns.

---

## Frame 3 — Measurement-Instrument Frame

**Position:** You ARE the measuring apparatus. You change what you measure; your precision limits what is knowable.

**What it reveals:** Observer effects, Heisenberg-type coupling between measurement and system, what the measurement itself costs, what cannot be measured simultaneously.

**Embedding template:**
```
Measurement-instrument perspective: I am the tool used to measure [property X].
My action of measuring changes [what? how?].
I have finite resolution of [precision level].
What I cannot distinguish: [below-threshold differences].
What my measurement COSTS the system: [energy, time, perturbation].
Can [property X] and [property Y] be measured simultaneously? If not, why not?
Limit case of measurement: What happens when [measurement precision → 0 or → ∞]?
```

**Typical findings:** Coupling between action and observation, measurement overhead that is not accounted for, fundamental limits on what can be known simultaneously.

---

## Usage Protocol (S4 Step 2)

Apply all ≥3 frames to the system model from S1 primitives. Run them independently, then compare:

1. **Cross-frame conflicts:** Where do two frames predict different behavior? These are theory collision candidates.
2. **Frame-exclusive findings:** What appears in only one frame? These are the most valuable insights (visible only from that position).
3. **Frame-independent findings:** What appears in all frames? These are the most robust structural facts.

**Output:** For each frame: narrative description of what is visible + at least one limit case + any theory collision with another frame.

---

## Adding Domain-Specific Frames (Optional)

If the problem has an obvious additional observer position (regulatory observer, end-user, adversary, future operator), embed it in the same format. Maximum additional frames: 2. Label them clearly.
